Re: Submission of original Article: “Domain-gated latent diffusion discovers novel HMX-class energetic materials with first-principles validation”

Dear Editor,

We submit for your consideration our manuscript, “Domain-gated latent diffusion discovers novel HMX-class energetic materials with first-principles validation.” The work presents a generative machine-learning recipe that produces twelve novel high-performance energetic compounds, each confirmed by density-functional theory against six experimental anchors.

No new HMX-class energetic material has been disclosed in fifteen years, yet performance gains in this regime translate directly to reduced propellant mass, smaller warheads, more efficient civilian gas-generators such as automotive airbags, and safer mining and demolition operations. Generative machine learning has redrawn pharmaceutical and small-molecule design, but the central premise of those successes, a deep well of trustworthy property measurements, does not hold for explosives chemistry. Of roughly 66,000 labelled CHNO molecules with reported detonation properties, only about 3,000 carry experimental or DFT-quality values; the remaining stratum of literature-derived and surrogate labels is too noisy to drive a generator and too informative to discard. Standard recipes either memorise the high-performance tail or extrapolate without calibration.

Domain-gated latent diffusion (DGLD) turns this label-quality stratification from a liability into a usable training signal. A four-tier label-trust gate routes only the trusted tier into the conditional-gradient branch; noisier tiers train the unconditional prior via classifier-free guidance dropout. A multi-task score model adds independently-switchable sample-time steering on viability, sensitivity, and hazard. Every candidate passes a four-stage validation funnel ending in B3LYP/$\omega$B97X-D3BJ density-functional theory, calibrated against six experimental anchors. DGLD yields twelve DFT-confirmed novel leads at calibrated densities 1.84–2.09 g/cm3 and Kamlet–Jacobs detonation velocities in the HMX class. The headline compound, 3,4,5-trinitro-1,2-isoxazole, reaches 2.09 g/cm3 and 8.25 km/s while remaining structurally dissimilar from the entire 65,980-row labelled corpus (max-Tanimoto 0.27) and absent from PubChem.

Against four no-diffusion baselines run on the same corpus (SMILES-LSTM, MolMIM 70 M, REINVENT 4, SELFIES-GA), DGLD is the only method that produces molecules simultaneously novel and on-target under first-principles validation. SMILES-LSTM exact-rediscovers training molecules; MolMIM is novel but below HMX; REINVENT 4 peaks at 9.02 km/s; SELFIES-GA shows a 3.5 km/s surrogate-to-DFT collapse. The tier-gating mechanism is in principle domain-agnostic: any generative-ML problem with stratified label trust (drug discovery, where bioactivity sits above QSAR; computational materials, where measured sits above computed sits above inferred) should admit the same recipe with only the validation funnel changing. End-to-end reproduction fits within roughly 45 GPU-hours on commodity datacentre instances; all code, data, and trained model checkpoints are released under permissive open licences.

This work sits at Nature Machine Intelligence's intersection of methodological advance and physical-sciences impact. It contributes both a generative-ML recipe (label-quality gating in latent diffusion with switchable multi-task sample-time steering) and a substantive domain result (the first HMX-class lead set produced by a generative model and confirmed by first-principles audit). It should interest readers working on generative models for the physical sciences, on label-noise-aware training under distributional sparsity, and on the broader project of moving machine-learning-driven materials discovery from in-silico screening to first-principles-validated leads.
